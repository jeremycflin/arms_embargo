Are Arms Embargoes Really Effective?

A cloer look into arms transfers, embargoes and conflicts

Produced by Jeremy C.F. Lin

China

In the 80s, China's arms import partners were the United States, France, West Germany, Italy, and Canada. It received 171 millions worth of weapons in total.

Following the bloody government crackdown on Tiananmen demonstration in 1989, the European Union announced an arms embargo against China.

However, the European Union did not set a common position on which arms items would be subject to the arms embargo. And members (see colored areas) interpreted the embargo differently.

In 1990, right after the announcement of the EU embargo, Russia (see dark grey) started to export its arms to China aggressively, and quickly became China's biggest arms import parter.

Even now, the European Union embargo is still ongoing, but ineffective.

Myanmar

In early 90s, Myanmar's top arms importer was China. Its second biggest arms import parter was Poland (see colored area).

In 1991, the European Union imposed an arms embargo against Myanmar.

This embargo covered arms, munitions and military equipment as well as equipmentt that might be used for internal repression or terrorism.

Though Poland was not in the European Union, its arms exports to Myanmar drastically dropped.

And then Russia (see dark grey) stepped in and became Myanmar's largest arms importer.

Though most economic sanctions were lifted on the Burmese government, the arms embargo was maintained till toady.

North Korea

From the 80s to early 00s, most of North Korea's arms imports parters have been communism influenced countries, including China, Kaxakhstan, Russia and Soviet Union.

In 2006, both the United Nations and the European Union established arms embargos against North Korea.

These arms embargoes were rather effective, though Russia was still exporting a small amount of arms to North Korea till 2010.

Syria

From the 80s to the beginning of 00s, the Soviet Union was selling an astonishing amount of arms to Syria, total valued 15,546 millions. Czechoslovakia was also a close arms trade partner to Syria. It sold Syria 696 millions worth of arms.

After the dissolution of the Soviet Union in 1991, North Korea jumped in and traded Syria 472 millions worth of arms while Russia remained active in arms transfers with Syria.

In 2011, in response to the unrest, the European Union imposed a full arms embargo against Syria. The League of Arab States announced economic and financial sanctions as well as an arms embargo on Syria in the same year.

Even though Russia was not subject to the embargo on Syria, its arms sales to Syria jumped from 282 millions in 2011 to 351 millions in 2012 and 2013.

Both the European Union and The League of Arab States's embargoes against Syria remained ongoing till today.
